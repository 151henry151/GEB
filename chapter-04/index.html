Chapter IV: Consistency, Completeness, and Geometry

Some rules never fight each other: you can't prove something and also prove the opposite. That's called being consistent. Other rules are complete if whenever something is true, you can prove it with the rules.

People used to think there was only one way to do geometry. Then they found other geometries that also don't fight themselves but look different (curved space). So the same words (line, point) can mean different things in different worlds.

Consistency and completeness

A formal system is consistent if you never can prove both a statement and its opposite. It's complete if every true statement (in the intended interpretation) is provable.

Geometry and undefined terms

Euclidean geometry uses point, line, plane without defining them. Non-Euclidean geometry uses the same kind of axioms but with a different interpretation (e.g. line as a great circle on a sphere), and it's still consistent.

Why it matters for Gödel

Later we'll see that rich systems like arithmetic can't be both consistent and complete: some true statements won't be provable.

Consistency, completeness, and undefined terms

Hofstadter explicates the Contracrostipunctus dialogue: consistency (no contradiction) and completeness (every truth provable). He ties this to the history of Euclidean and non-Euclidean geometry. Terms like point and line are undefined; their meaning is fixed by the axioms and the intended interpretation.

Formal systems and interpretation

Multiple interpretations can satisfy the same axioms; consistency is a property of the system under an interpretation. This sets up the idea that truth and provability might come apart when the system is rich enough.
