Chapter VII: The Propositional Calculus

You can play a game with "and," "or," and "not." If I say "it's rainy AND cold," the whole thing is only true when both parts are true. "Rainy OR sunny" is true when at least one is true. "NOT rainy" is true when it's not rainy.

People made strict rules for this game so that you never get a wrong answer if you follow the rules. The rules are the propositional calculus. The same idea as the pq-system: follow the shapes, and truth comes out.

Propositional logic

The propositional calculus is a formal system for combining statements with and, or, not, if-then. Each letter (P, Q) can be true or false. The rules tell you which combined formulas are theorems (always true, i.e. tautologies).

Form and meaning again

When we interpret P, Q as statements and the connectives as and, or, not, the theorems turn out to be exactly the logical truths. So meaning emerges from form.

The propositional calculus as a formal system

Hofstadter presents the propositional calculus: a formal language with proposition letters and connectives (and, or, not, if-then), axioms (e.g. tautologies), and rules of inference (e.g. modus ponens). Theorems are exactly the tautologies.

Isomorphism with truth values

Under the standard interpretation, the theorems correspond to logical truths. So once again, meaningless symbols acquire meaning by virtue of the form of the theorems.
